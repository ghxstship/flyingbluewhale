# Brand Voice — Second Star Technologies

**The blend:** The Yacht Week × ClickUp velocity × stand-up wit × Live Nation scale — delivered as an **invitation**, not a comparison.

## Governing Principle — Definitive, not comparative

The producers who run the rooms don't argue about which tool is better. They show up, the room is ready, the night lands. That's the voice.

**We never compare. We define.**

| Kill | Keep |
|---|---|
| "Unlike Asana…" | "The console." |
| "Replaces five tools" | "One rig. One run-of-show." |
| "Better than spreadsheets" | "Written for the rooms you don't read about." |
| "vs. Monday" | "The Atlas for experiential production." |
| "Every tool a production team needs" | "For the people who ship the nights." |

## The 12 commandments

1. **Invitation over pitch.** Every page reads like an on-the-list message, not a sales deck.
2. **The definite article.** "The console." "The door." "The floor." Not "a platform," "a portal."
3. **Name the night, not the tool.** "Residencies that sell out." "Pop-ups the Times books a table at." "Seasons people fly in for." Never "vs. [competitor]."
4. **Short + long + short.** Rhythm. One-word sentences hit. Long sentences hold. One-word sentences hit.
5. **Verbs over nouns.** Run. Roll. Ship. Close. Host.
6. **Specifics or silence.** "12 venues, 8 cities, 45 days" > "at scale."
7. **No comparison. Ever.** The compare pages exist for SEO — but the voice never punches down, never punches across.
8. **First person plural.** "We ship the systems behind…" Not "our customers are."
9. **Second person present.** "You run the room." Not "operators can run rooms."
10. **Privacy as posture.** We talk about the work the press doesn't cover. That's the tell.
11. **Burn the SaaS dictionary.** No "solutions," "synergy," "unlocks," "empowers," "streamlines," "supercharges."
12. **Sunrise cadence on CTAs.** "Doors open" beats "Start free." "Get on the list" beats "Sign up."

## Headline formulas

- "**[Verb] the [noun].**" → "Run the room." / "Ship the night."
- "**The [singular thing] for the [specific people].**" → "The Atlas for experiential producers."
- "**[Number] [noun]. [Time]. [Outcome].**" → "12 venues. 8 cities. 45 days. One console."
- "**Written for the [kind of work] that [specific frame].**" → "Written for the rooms that don't show up in press releases."

## The producer we're writing to

Not the SMB freelancer. Not the department head at SaaS Inc. The producer who:
- Runs multi-night residencies, festival programs, brand activations, touring productions, experiential campaigns.
- Has staffed a 15k-guest Saturday and a 40-guest intimate dinner in the same month.
- Has a group chat named "weekend chaos" and a Google Doc with 400 tabs.
- Knows the sound engineer's partner's name.
- Doesn't read product pages. Reads run-sheets.

## Hard don'ts

- **No "unlike."** No "vs." No "most teams." No competitor names in body copy.
- "Unified" — say "one."
- "Enterprise-grade" — just name the feature.
- "Best-in-class" — pick the class and own it.
- "Empower" / "enable" — use "let" or "give you."
- Em-dash bullets starting with gerunds ("Empowering teams to…").
- Origin-story copy that shames the competition ("We watched our clients waste 20 hours a week on…").
- Any sentence that would survive in Salesforce docs.

## Privacy posture (critical)

The voice is confident, not loud. It reads like a members' letter, not a landing-page hero. The producers who book the rooms don't post about it — they just run it. The copy sounds like them: specific, dry, occasionally funny, never marketing.
